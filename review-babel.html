The Queer Book Nook

Babel

R.F. Kuang

In the years to come, Robin would return so many times to this night. He was forever astonished by its mysterious alchemy, by how easily two badly socialized, restrictively raised strangers had transformed into kindred spirits in a span of minutes.

'Babel' by R.F. Kuang is a captivating tale that delves into the complex issues of colonialism and identity, weaving together a narrative that is both engaging and thought-provoking. One of the book's strengths lies in its well-crafted characters, although there are instances where they may feel a bit flat.

The romantic development between Robin and Ramy, while a commendable attempt at representing LGBTQ+ people of color, seemed somewhat overshadowed and lacked the depth that the rest of the narrative exuded. It felt like a secondary element, leaving me yearning for a more profound exploration of their relationship. Therefore, this book should not be recommended as an 'LGBT+ book' and gladly, the book's promotion does not rely on its LGBT+ inclusion.

Nevertheless, the novel shines in its deeper interrogation of the implications of colonialism. Kuang skillfully explores the challenges faced by immigrants and those stolen from their native lands as they struggle to assimilate into new environments. The author's portrayal of this struggle is poignant and provides a necessary reflection on the difficulties faced by individuals grappling with their stolen heritage.

The book's tone, while at times direct, is well-suited to the overall narrative. It might be argued that the message could have been delivered in more nuanced ways, but the straightforward approach aligns with the book's atmosphere and does not detract from its impact.

A standout feature of 'Babel' is the incorporation of footnotes, showcasing Kuang's impressive linguistic knowledge. These moments elevate the book, making it feel like a meticulously executed thesis. While some may argue that it veers towards a more academic style, these footnotes were the most captivating aspects and allowed the reader to connect with the author themselves. Kuang's skill in this area suggests a potential for equally compelling non-fiction works on language.

'Babel' is a book that successfully tackles significant themes such as colonialism and identity. While there are moments that could benefit from more depth, the novel's strengths lie in its exploration of complex issues, and the author's linguistic prowess. Kuang's potential for non-fiction writing in the realm of language is evident and adds an extra layer of appreciation for this already thought-provoking work.

about the author

Rebecca F. Kuangis a Chinese-American author born on February 3, 1996. She gained acclaim for her debut trilogy, "The Poppy War," which she started writing during her undergraduate studies at Georgetown University. Kuang completed her Master's degree in Chinese Studies at the University of Cambridge, focusing on 20th-century Chinese military history. Her academic background deeply influences the intricate world-building and historical nuances present in her novels.
